In commercial office buildings, heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems account for a significant share of total energy consumption.

(Department of the Environment and Energy, 2013)

Yet most HVAC systems operate at a constant rate, ventilating as if every room is always full, regardless of real-time occupancy.

This leads to over-ventilation during quiet hours and under-ventilation when spaces are crowded.

This mismatch results in:

Wasted Energy

Ventilating empty or lightly used rooms consumes unnecessary electricity. Over time, this leads to significant energy waste, driving up operational costs without delivering added benefit.

Inconsistent Air Quality

When the system doesn't adjust to rising occupancy, indoor air can become stale and CO₂ levels can rise above healthy limits. This impacts comfort, concentration, and overall well-being for building occupants.

What if your building could breathe only when it needs to?

Demand-Control Ventilation makes that possible

Instead of running at a constant rate, DCV adjusts airflow based on real-time occupancy and indoor air quality, ensuring each space gets just the right amount of fresh air.

At the heart of this system is the CO2 sensor, which tells the ventilation system when and how much to operate.

Why Monitor CO₂?

Every time people breathe, they release carbon dioxide (CO₂) into the air. In closed spaces, CO₂ levels can build up quickly when many people are present and there’s not enough fresh air. That makes CO₂ a powerful indicator of two things:

Occupancy Levels

Higher CO₂ means more people are in the room. Lower CO₂ usually means the space is empty or lightly used.

By monitoring CO₂, DCV systems can estimate how many people are using the space without needing invasive cameras or motion detectors.

Ventilation Performance

When CO₂ levels rise too high, it means that fresh air is not circulating fast enough. This can lead to headaches, tiredness, and poor concentration.

DCV uses this data to increase airflow when needed and scale back when it's not, maintaining both comfort and air quality.
